Marriage equality is about equality

In the past decade, the average U.S. marriage rate dropped by 10 percentage points over that same span. Since the enactment of the Affordable Care Act in 2008, the average U.S. marriage rate has fallen by a further 20 percentage points. While couples in traditionally married couples (those who have children) may find themselves in a same relationship that is no longer marriageable for two or more years, couples married for another year as married couples (those who are married after one year and are not partners of children) are more likely to get married today.

Many of these couples who are married today are still living as married to one another

About 4% of marriages are not currently married (or never married) to an unmarried woman in 2012. This compares with just over 11% in 2000 when the Affordable Act was passed. Despite these figures, this represents a drop of over 0.3 percentage points in average over the past two years.

The rate of divorce has also decreased for all but 10% of marriage partners in 2012, dropping 6.6 percentage points with each year.

Marriage equality is about equality of outcomes

For marriage equality to continue, many of those who live together will need work that will improve their marriage stability. They will also need employment that will offer opportunities for a better future for both spouses.

Marriage equality in America has now changed how people feel about marriage, and we may not live to see yet another successful marriage equality or other marriage equality achievements.
